Fahrenheit 451
by Ray Bradbury
Genre: Science Fiction
Reading Fahrenheit 451 felt like stepping into a chilling mirror of our own world—one where books are banned, ideas are dangerous, and screens have replaced meaningful conversations. What struck me most wasn’t just the dystopian setting, but the quiet desperation of the main character, Montag, as he starts to question everything he's been told to destroy. Bradbury doesn’t just warn us about censorship—he dives deep into what makes us human: curiosity, emotion, the hunger to understand. The language is poetic but piercing, and though the book is short, its impact is lasting. It’s one of those stories that lingers, making you pause before turning on the TV or scrolling your phone. It reminded me why books matter—why stories matter.
About the Author
Ray Bradbury (1920–2012) was an American author and visionary best known for his work in science fiction and fantasy. With novels like Fahrenheit 451 and short stories like those in The Martian Chronicles, he blended imagination with sharp social commentary. Bradbury had a deep love for libraries, storytelling, and the power of ideas—values that shine through in his writing. He believed in the beauty of curiosity and the importance of questioning the world around us, leaving behind a legacy that continues to inspire readers across generations.
